<section class="paper-card paper-card--featured">
  <div class="paper-card__meta">
    <span>Job Market Paper</span>
  </div>
  <div class="paper-card__content">
    <h2>Dominant Followership in AI-Driven Algorithmic Pricing</h2>
    <h3>Abstract</h3>
    <p>
      Pricing algorithms allow firms to monitor and respond to rival price
      changes at scale. This paper studies how such responsiveness affects
      competition when firms differ in demand-side market power. Using
      high-frequency cross-platform pricing data, I identify sequential price
      adjustments and measure firms' responses to rivals' earlier price
      changes. Amazon's behavior is distinct: rival changes predict its later
      adjustments, especially same-direction responses after rival increases.
    </p>
    <p>
      To investigate how this pattern affects prices, I develop a model of
      dominant followership. I find that a demand-advantaged follower may match
      rather than undercut rival price increases to monetize its existing
      customer base. This mechanism raises prices only when the
      demand-advantaged firm moves second. In Q-learning simulations, the same
      timing condition determines long-run outcomes: after observing rival
      price changes, the demand-advantaged firm learns response rules that
      sustain higher prices and reproduce Amazon's tendency to follow rival
      increases without pricing above them.
    </p>
  </div>
</section>

  <div class="paper-card__content">
    <h2>Consumer-Side AI Shopping Agents and Algorithmic Pricing</h2>
    <p class="paper-card__status">Work in progress</p>
    <p>
      This project studies how consumer-side AI shopping agents change
      competition when firms use pricing algorithms. It examines whether
      agents' access to cross-platform price histories erodes dominant
      platforms' demand advantages by lowering search frictions, or whether
      default position and loyalty allow those advantages to persist.
    </p>
